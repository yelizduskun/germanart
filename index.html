A Survey of Style

Art Movements

Overview

From Gothic Altarpieces
to Expressionist Fire

German painting spans the devotional grandeur of the late Middle Ages through the radical experiments of the twentieth century.

I
Gothic & Late Gothic
c. 1300 – 1500

Characterised by elongated figures, gold leaf, and intense religious devotion. Altarpiece painting flourished in the workshops of Cologne, Nuremberg, and the Rhineland, bridging Byzantine formalism and emerging naturalism.

II
Northern Renaissance
c. 1430 – 1580

Dürer and Cranach brought Italian perspective and humanism into dialogue with German devotional traditions. Oil painting, portraiture, and printmaking transformed the visual culture of the Holy Roman Empire.

III
Baroque & Rococo
c. 1600 – 1770

The devastation of the Thirty Years' War disrupted German art, but the courts of Dresden, Vienna, and Munich produced magnificent ceiling paintings and intimate cabinet pictures in the Catholic Baroque tradition.

IV
Romanticism
c. 1790 – 1860

Friedrich, Runge, and the Nazarenes made Germany the world capital of Romantic landscape and allegory. The infinite horizon, the lone wanderer, the cathedral in ruins — icons of a spiritual longing unique to the German tradition.

V
Realism & Historicism
c. 1840 – 1900

The Düsseldorf School and later Liebermann's social realism turned to peasant life, labour, and contemporary scenes. Adolph Menzel brought industrialisation onto the canvas with arresting precision.

VI
Expressionism
c. 1905 – 1933

Die Brücke and Der Blaue Reiter shattered convention with raw colour, distorted form, and existential urgency. Kirchner, Marc, Nolde, and Beckmann gave voice to a generation shaken by modernity, war, and psychic unrest.

VII
Neue Sachlichkeit
c. 1920 – 1933

New Objectivity responded to Expressionism's excess with brutal clarity. Otto Dix and George Grosz dissected Weimar society — its veterans, prostitutes, and profiteers — with sardonic, unflinching realism.

VIII
Post-War & Neo-Expressionism
c. 1960 – present

Anselm Kiefer, Gerhard Richter, and Sigmar Polke grappled with history, memory, and identity after the Holocaust. The Neue Wilde of the 1980s revived painterly gesture, while Richter's photo-paintings blurred the borders of perception.

15th – 16th Century

The Renaissance Masters

Self-Portrait at 28 (1500)
Albrecht Dürer
1471 – 1528 · Nuremberg

The supreme master of the Northern Renaissance, Dürer fused Italian Renaissance clarity with Northern European detail. His self-portraits are among the most psychologically penetrating in all of Western art, and his theoretical writings on proportion shaped centuries of practice.

Key works: Self-Portrait at 28 (1500), Four Apostles (1526), Adoration of the Magi (1504)

Northern Renaissance
The Ill-Matched Couple (c. 1532)
Lucas Cranach the Elder
1472 – 1553 · Kronach & Wittenberg

Court painter to the Electors of Saxony and close friend of Martin Luther, Cranach shaped the visual language of the Protestant Reformation. His slender, sensuous figures — whether biblical heroines or mythological nymphs — are instantly recognisable.

Key works: Venus and Cupid (1509), Adam and Eve (1526), portraits of Martin Luther

Northern Renaissance
Meeting of St. Erasmus and St. Maurice (c. 1520)
Matthias Grünewald
c. 1470 – 1528

Grünewald's Isenheim Altarpiece stands as one of the most harrowing and transcendent paintings in Western history. Working outside the rationalism of the Italian Renaissance, he pursued an ecstatic, proto-Expressionist intensity of suffering and resurrection.

Key works: Isenheim Altarpiece (c. 1512–1516), Crucifixion (c. 1524)

Late Gothic / Northern Renaissance
The Ambassadors (1533)
Hans Holbein the Younger
c. 1497 – 1543 · Augsburg & London

The greatest portraitist of the sixteenth century, Holbein documented the Tudor court with uncanny precision. His paintings of Henry VIII, Erasmus, and the German merchants of London's Steelyard reveal his mastery of surface, character, and symbolic detail.

Key works: The Ambassadors (1533), Portrait of Erasmus (1523), The Body of the Dead Christ in the Tomb (1521)

Northern Renaissance
Late 18th – Mid 19th Century

German Romanticism

Wanderer above the Sea of Fog (1818)
Caspar David Friedrich
1774 – 1840 · Greifswald & Dresden

The greatest painter of the German Romantic movement, Friedrich transformed landscape into a meditation on mortality, faith, and the sublime. The lone figure gazing into fog or infinite ocean — the Rückenfigur — became the defining image of Romantic interiority.

Key works: Wanderer above the Sea of Fog (1818), The Monk by the Sea (1810), Abbey in the Oakwood (1810)

Romanticism
Self-Portrait (c. 1805)
Philipp Otto Runge
1777 – 1810 · Hamburg

Runge's short life produced an extraordinary body of symbolic work. His unfinished cycle The Times of Day and his vibrant, strange portraits of children proposed a new mythology of nature, colour, and spirit that anticipated Symbolism by half a century.

Key works: Morning (1808), The Hülsenbeck Children (1805–06), The Lesson of the Nightingale (1804–05)

Romanticism
Castle by the Sea at Moonrise (1831)
Karl Friedrich Schinkel
1781 – 1841 · Berlin

Best known as Prussia's master architect, Schinkel was also a visionary painter. His panoramic stage-set designs and Romantic landscapes blend Gothic architecture with idealised Mediterranean light, imagining a world suspended between antiquity and the modern age.

Key works: Gothic Cathedral above a River (1813), Castle by the Sea at Moonrise (1831)

Romanticism / Neoclassicism
Mid – Late 19th Century

Realism & the Düsseldorf School

The Balcony Room (1845)
Adolph Menzel
1815 – 1905 · Berlin

Menzel painted Frederick the Great's Prussia, Berlin's iron foundries, and the teeming streets of imperial Germany with equal vigour. His intimate, light-drenched interiors anticipate Impressionism, while his industrial scenes — particularly The Iron Rolling Mill — rank among the most powerful images of the 19th century.

Key works: The Balcony Room (1845), Flute Concert of Frederick the Great (1852), Balcony Room (1845)

Realism
Free Hour in the Amsterdam Orphanage (1881)
Max Liebermann
1847 – 1935 · Berlin

The leading German Impressionist and founder of the Berlin Secession, Liebermann captured peasant workers, beach scenes, and bourgeois leisure with loose, luminous brushwork. As a Jewish artist who lived into the Nazi era, his fate embodied the catastrophe of 20th-century German history.

Key works: Women Plucking Geese (1872), Free Hour in the Amsterdam Orphanage (1881), Self-Portrait (1925)

Impressionism / Realism
1905 – 1933

German Expressionism

Street, Berlin (1913)
Ernst Ludwig Kirchner
1880 – 1938 · Aschaffenburg

Founding member of Die Brücke, Kirchner's jagged cityscapes and figures pulsate with nervous energy. His Berlin street scenes — angular, lurid, anonymous — rank among the definitive images of urban modernity. Declared "degenerate" by the Nazis, he destroyed hundreds of his works before taking his own life.

Key works: Street, Berlin (1913), Five Women in the Street (1913), Self-Portrait as a Soldier (1915)

Expressionism · Die Brücke
Blue Horse I (1911)
Franz Marc
1880 – 1916 · Munich

Co-founder of Der Blaue Reiter with Wassily Kandinsky, Marc sought a spiritual truth through colour and animal subjects. His blue horses and yellow tigers are icons of German Expressionism. He was killed on the Western Front at Verdun, aged 36.

Key works: Blue Horse I (1911), Blue Horse I (1911), Fate of the Animals (1913)

Expressionism · Der Blaue Reiter
Masks (1911)
Emil Nolde
1867 – 1956 · Schleswig

Nolde's incandescent colour — acid yellows, bruised purples, luminous reds — gave his religious subjects and North Sea landscapes an almost hallucinatory power. His relationship with National Socialism remains deeply controversial despite his own works being condemned as "degenerate."

Key works: Masks (1911), The Last Supper (1909), Marsh Landscape (1916)

Expressionism
Portrait of the Journalist Sylvia von Harden (1926)
Otto Dix
1891 – 1969 · Gera

A veteran of the Western Front, Dix documented the horror of World War I and the moral decay of Weimar Germany with merciless clarity. His triptych Der Krieg is among the most devastating anti-war statements in Western painting. His portraits strip their subjects with surgical precision.

Key works: The War (1929–32), Trench Warfare (1923), Metropolis (1928), Portrait of the Journalist Sylvia von Harden (1926)

Neue Sachlichkeit
Republican Automatons (1920)
George Grosz
1893 – 1959 · Berlin

Grosz's savage caricatures of Weimar Berlin — fat industrialists, wounded veterans, leering profiteers — made him the sharpest political painter of his generation. A committed satirist and Dadaist, he was tried multiple times for blasphemy and obscenity before emigrating to the USA in 1933.

Key works: Eclipse of the Sun (1926), Republican Automatons (1920), The Funeral (1917–18)

Neue Sachlichkeit · Dada
Self-Portrait with Violin (1926)
Max Beckmann
1884 – 1950 · Leipzig

Beckmann's monumental triptychs — epic, allegorical, densely packed — are among the most ambitious paintings of the 20th century. His response to World War I, the rise of Nazism, and exile produced a deeply personal mythology of human suffering and transcendence.

Key works: Self-Portrait with Violin (1926), Night (1918–19), Self-Portrait with Saxophone (1930)

Expressionism / Neue Sachlichkeit
1960 – Present

Post-War & Contemporary

Margarethe (1981)
Anselm Kiefer
b. 1945 · Donaueschingen

Kiefer's monumental, material-laden canvases — burned, straw-encrusted, coated in lead — confront Germany's Nazi past with unflinching directness. Drawing on mythology, Kabbalah, and history, his work transforms the wound of the 20th century into landscape-scale elegy.

Key works: Margarethe (1981), Margarethe / Shulamite (1981), The Orders of the Night (1996)

Neo-Expressionism
Abstract Painting (1992)
Gerhard Richter
b. 1932 · Dresden

Perhaps the most influential painter alive, Richter has oscillated his entire career between photorealistic grey paintings blurred to the edge of visibility and radiant abstract colour fields. His series October 18, 1977 — blurred photographs of the Baader-Meinhof terrorists — epitomises painting as a meditation on history and seeing.

Key works: October 18, 1977 cycle (1988), Abstract Painting (1992), Abstract Painting 724 (1990)

1500 – Present

Five Centuries of German Art

Chronology

A Timeline of German Painting

Key works, movements, and political upheavals that shaped five centuries of German art — from the Reformation to the present day.

c. 1503–1528
Dürer and the Northern Renaissance

Albrecht Dürer transforms German art by fusing Italian Renaissance proportion with Northern European detail. His self-portraits, engravings, and theoretical writings on human proportion establish the artist as an intellectual equal of the great Italian masters. Lucas Cranach and Hans Holbein follow, making Germany a centre of humanist painting.

1517–1555
The Reformation and Protestant Art

Martin Luther's Reformation upends the religious patronage that had sustained painting across Europe. Cranach, Luther's close friend and court painter in Wittenberg, creates the visual language of Protestantism — replacing Catholic saints with biblical narrative, portraiture, and reformist allegory. The destruction of altarpieces and religious images in iconoclast riots reshapes what German painters are asked to paint.

c. 1512–1516
The Isenheim Altarpiece

Matthias Grünewald completes his devastating masterpiece for an Antonite monastery treating skin disease. Its tormented Crucifixion — Christ's body covered in plague-like lesions — and radiant Resurrection are among the most emotionally extreme paintings in Western history, working entirely outside the Renaissance rationalism of his contemporaries.

1618–1648
The Thirty Years' War: Destruction and Silence

The most devastating war in European history until the 20th century kills a third of the German population and obliterates the artistic infrastructure of the Holy Roman Empire. Workshops close, patronage collapses, and Germany falls a generation behind the flourishing Baroque courts of France, Spain, and the Netherlands. The trauma creates a cultural rupture that shapes German art for over a century.

c. 1795–1850
German Romanticism: Friedrich and the Sublime

Caspar David Friedrich, Philipp Otto Runge, and the Nazarene Brotherhood make Germany the world capital of Romantic painting. Friedrich's lone figures confronting infinite fog, ocean, and mountain — the Rückenfigur — become the defining images of Romantic interiority and spiritual longing. The movement responds directly to Napoleonic occupation and the search for a German national identity through landscape and nature.

1815–1848
The Nazarenes and the Düsseldorf School

The Brotherhood of St. Luke — known as the Nazarenes — retreat to Rome to revive the spiritual sincerity of pre-Renaissance painting. Back in Germany, the Düsseldorf Academy becomes the most influential art school in the world, attracting students from across Europe and America. Its blend of technical rigour, history painting, and landscape defines academic German art throughout the 19th century.

1848–1900
Realism, Industrialisation, and the Berlin Secession

Adolph Menzel paints Frederick the Great's Prussia and Berlin's iron foundries with photographic intensity. Max Liebermann, deeply influenced by French Impressionism and Dutch realism, brings a new social vision to German painting — depicting peasants, orphans, and urban leisure. In 1898 Liebermann founds the Berlin Secession, breaking with the conservative academic establishment and opening Germany to international modernism.

1905
Die Brücke — The Bridge

Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, Erich Heckel, Karl Schmidt-Rottluff, and Fritz Bleyl found Die Brücke (The Bridge) in Dresden — the first Expressionist group in Germany. Rejecting academic tradition and inspired by African and Oceanic art, van Gogh and Munch, they use raw colour, jagged line, and emotional distortion to express modern anxiety. The group relocates to Berlin in 1910, where Kirchner's electric street scenes become icons of pre-war urban alienation. Die Brücke dissolves in 1913.

1911
Der Blaue Reiter — The Blue Rider

Wassily Kandinsky and Franz Marc found Der Blaue Reiter in Munich, publishing their epoch-making almanac and holding exhibitions that juxtapose folk art, children's drawings, and avant-garde painting. Marc's blue horses and Kandinsky's near-abstract colour compositions propose a spiritual basis for art that transcends national boundaries. In 1914 the First World War destroys the group: Marc is killed at Verdun in 1916; Kandinsky returns to Russia.

1914–1918
The First World War: Art in the Trenches

The catastrophe of the Western Front transforms German art. Otto Dix serves as a machine-gunner and returns obsessively to the experience in drawings, prints, and finally his monumental triptych Der Krieg (1929–32). Max Beckmann is invalided out after a breakdown. The war kills Franz Marc, August Macke, and a generation of younger artists. The survivors emerge radicalized, their optimism shattered, their work darkened by trauma and rage.

1919–1923
Weimar Republic: Dada and Political Art

In the chaos of post-war Berlin — revolution, inflation, political assassination — German Dada erupts as a savage critique of the bourgeoisie and the military. George Grosz and John Heartfield produce ferocious photomontages and caricatures attacking the ruling classes. Grosz is tried multiple times for blasphemy and obscenity. The Bauhaus opens in Weimar in 1919, bringing art, craft, and design together under one radical roof.

1923–1933
Neue Sachlichkeit — New Objectivity

As Expressionism's emotional excess gives way to disillusionment, Neue Sachlichkeit (New Objectivity) emerges as its cold-eyed successor. Otto Dix and George Grosz dissect Weimar society — its veterans, prostitutes, war profiteers, and corrupt officials — with surgical, merciless realism. Dix's Portrait of the Journalist Sylvia von Harden (1926) and his series of war cripples are among the most confrontational images in 20th-century art.

1933
Hitler Takes Power — The Nazification of Art

On 30 January 1933, Adolf Hitler is appointed Chancellor of Germany. Within months the Nazi regime begins dismantling the entire structure of modern German art. The Bauhaus is forced to close. Jewish artists — including Max Liebermann, who resigns as president of the Prussian Academy of Arts — are expelled from institutions. Modernism is declared an expression of Jewish-Bolshevik cultural subversion. Liebermann, trapped in Berlin, dies in 1935; his wife takes her own life in 1943 to avoid deportation to a concentration camp.

1937
Entartete Kunst — Degenerate Art

The Nazi regime stages the Entartete Kunst (Degenerate Art) exhibition in Munich — the most visited art exhibition in history, attended by over two million people in four months. 650 works by Kirchner, Nolde, Marc, Beckmann, Grosz, Dix, and others are displayed with mocking labels as evidence of cultural decay, mental illness, and racial impurity. Over 16,000 works are confiscated from German museums in total. Many are sold abroad; others are burned. Kirchner, humiliated, destroys hundreds of his own works and takes his own life in 1938. Emil Nolde — himself a Nazi party member — has 1,052 works seized. Beckmann flees to Amsterdam the day after Hitler's opening speech.

1937–1945
Große Deutsche Kunstausstellung — The Nazi Ideal

Simultaneously with the Degenerate Art exhibition, the Nazis open the Große Deutsche Kunstausstellung (Great German Art Exhibition) in Munich's newly built House of German Art — a temple of neoclassical pomposity. The approved art: heroic nudes, peasant idylls, portraits of Hitler, and military scenes painted in a smooth academic realism that deliberately rejected every development in art since 1880. Eight such exhibitions are held annually until 1944.

1945–1960
Zero Hour: Art After the Holocaust

Germany emerges from the war physically and morally devastated. The concept of Stunde Null (Zero Hour) — the idea of starting culture from scratch — shapes the immediate post-war decades. Abstract art, suppressed under Nazism, enjoys a sudden rehabilitation as politically neutral. The divided nation produces two radically different art worlds: East Germany enforces Socialist Realism under Soviet doctrine; West Germany cautiously opens to American Abstract Expressionism and international modernism.

1961–1969
Fluxus, Zero Group, and the German Neo-Avant-Garde

The Zero group — Heinz Mack, Otto Piene, and Günther Uecker — seeks a new beginning through light, movement, and pure sensation, explicitly rejecting the expressive tradition tainted by Nazi appropriation. Joseph Beuys, the most influential German artist of the postwar era, begins his transformation of Fluxus performance into a mythological art of social healing. Gerhard Richter and Sigmar Polke coin the term "Capitalist Realism" as a sardonic German reply to American Pop Art.

1966–1988
Gerhard Richter: Photography and Painting

Gerhard Richter, who fled East Germany for Düsseldorf in 1961, develops his defining practice of painting over photographs blurred to the edge of visibility. Oscillating between photo-realism and radical abstraction, he questions every assumption about what painting is for. His cycle October 18, 1977 (1988) — fifteen paintings depicting the deaths of the Baader-Meinhof terrorists — becomes the defining German artwork of the postwar era: a meditation on history, violence, and the failure of representation.

1969–1993
Anselm Kiefer: Confronting the Nazi Past

Anselm Kiefer becomes the first major German artist to directly address the Nazi period in monumental painting. His early photographs of himself giving the Hitler salute in Europe cause outrage; his subsequent canvases — burned, lead-encrusted, straw-laden — transform the landscapes of German mythology and history into sites of guilt, mourning, and impossible redemption. Works like Margarethe and Shulamite (1981) take their titles from Paul Celan's Holocaust poem "Death Fugue."

1989
The Fall of the Wall: Reunification and New Identities

The fall of the Berlin Wall on 9 November 1989 reunites two art worlds that have developed in isolation for forty years. East German painters, trained in the figurative tradition of the Leipzig School, suddenly encounter the Western market. Neo-Expressionist painters such as Georg Baselitz, A.R. Penck, and Markus Lüpertz — many of whom fled the East in earlier decades — achieve international recognition. The reunified Germany grapples with how to memorialise both the Nazi and Communist pasts simultaneously.

1990s–2000s
The Leipzig School and New German Painting

The New Leipzig School — Neo Rauch, Tim Eitel, and others trained at the Leipzig Academy — emerges as the most commercially successful German art movement since Expressionism. Neo Rauch's dreamlike figurative canvases, blending Socialist Realist technique with surreal imagery, command prices in the millions and reopen international debate about the possibilities of narrative painting. Berlin, reunified and revitalised, becomes one of the world's leading centres for contemporary art.

2000–Present
German Art Today: Memory, Migration, and Abstraction

Contemporary German painting continues to wrestle with history while embracing new identities. Artists of Turkish-German heritage such as Hito Steyerl and Ayşe Erkmen expand the boundaries of what German art means in a multicultural society. Richter, now in his nineties, remains one of the most exhibited and debated artists in the world. The question of how to paint in Germany — after the Reformation, after Romanticism, after Expressionism, after Nazism, after the Wall — remains the defining challenge of the German artistic tradition.
